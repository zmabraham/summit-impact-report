A Moment the Field Was Ready For

The science, the tradition, and the practice — finally in one room.

For decades, mental-health care and spiritual life have run on parallel tracks — respectful of one another, but rarely in conversation. Clinicians sat with questions that reached beyond their frameworks: Why does this matter? How do I find meaning in suffering?

Research consistently shows that spirituality is one of the most powerful protective factors in mental health — not because it removes life's challenges, but because it helps people make meaning of them. Yet it remains one of the least-explored dimensions of clinical care.

Inside the Sessions

What the field actually heard

Across six hours, fifteen researchers and clinicians built a single argument: the wisdom of Jewish tradition, tested against modern science, belongs in the therapy room.

01

From antagonism to evidence

For a century the field followed Freud, who dismissed religion as a "mass delusion." The summit retired that posture — thousands of peer-reviewed studies now frame spirituality as a measurable clinical resource, and an ethical mandate.

02

Spirituality is innate — and visible in the brain

Lisa Miller's research locates a natural spiritual capacity that is roughly one-third inborn, two-thirds cultivated — and links a sustained spiritual life to dramatically lower rates of addiction, depression, and suicide.

03

Struggle as a doorway, not a defect

Speaker after speaker reframed depression, trauma, and doubt as openings for growth — and named the clinician's task: to recognize spiritual injury and meet it with spiritual engagement, never bypass.
